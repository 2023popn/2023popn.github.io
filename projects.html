01 / Battery Systems

Half-Pack Battery

Purdue EV Grand Prix — Design Lead
● Complete — 1st place, efficiency
The Constraint

Our EV Grand Prix competition car was carrying more battery pack than the car — or the rules — needed. Every extra kilogram of pack came directly out of the car's efficiency score, and the existing enclosure design was built around off-the-shelf brackets that didn't match our actual cell layout.

The Approach

I redesigned the pack enclosure and structural mounting from scratch, rather than adapting the old one. Structural brackets and cell retention features that didn't need to bear major load were moved to 3D-printed components, while load-bearing members stayed in sheet-formed aluminum — splitting the structure by which parts actually needed metal and which just needed to hold shape.

I hand-fabricated the sheet metal enclosure (cutting, bending, and fastening), then iterated the printed brackets over several revisions to dial in tolerances against the real cells rather than datasheet dimensions.

The Result

The finished pack came in at roughly half the weight of the previous design (##.# kg vs ##.# kg) while keeping full capacity (## kWh, ## cells), and the car placed 1st in efficiency at competition (X% ahead of 2nd place). It's the project I'd point to first for evidence I can take a system from "what if we cut mass here" to a physically validated part.

02 / Robotics & Mechanisms

SCARA Plasma Cutter

Multi-University Industry Collaboration
● Ongoing
The Constraint

An industry partner needed a low-cost SCARA-configuration arm capable of guiding a plasma cutting head across a work surface with repeatable precision — a project scoped and coordinated across multiple university teams, meaning the mechanical spec had to be agreed on before any team could start building independently.

The Approach

I've worked on the 2-degree-of-freedom mechanical arm design: joint geometry, link proportions, and the tradeoffs between reach, rigidity, and the plasma torch's own weight and heat. Because the build is split across schools, a lot of the work has been making sure the mechanical interface points are unambiguous enough that another team's electronics or controls work can bolt on without surprises.

The Result (so far)

The arm has gone through several prototype revisions (rev. X) as tolerances and torch clearance issues surfaced, with positioning repeatability currently around ±#.# mm and a working reach of ## cm. This one's still active — I'll update this section with final performance numbers (cycle time, payload rating, cost per unit) once the current build phase wraps.

03 / Marine Propulsion

Electric Paddleboard Fin

Independent Build
● Complete
The Constraint

I wanted powered thrust on a standard paddleboard without bolting on an obviously separate motor unit — which meant fitting a brushless DC motor and its battery into roughly the volume of a fin, and doing it in a housing that would be fully submerged and still keep water out of anything electrical.

The Approach

I built the housing around a brushless DC motor with a sealed battery compartment, working through gasket selection, potting, and cable-entry sealing to get a reliable waterproof boundary in a shape that still had to cut cleanly through water. Component placement was its own puzzle — everything had to fit inside a fin's footprint, with weight distributed so it didn't fight the board's handling.

The Result

A working sealed, motorized fin that mounts like a standard fin box component, producing roughly # lbs of thrust and a top assisted speed around # mph, with a runtime of about ## minutes per charge. It's a small build, but it's a complete one — motor selection, sealing, battery integration, and mounting, solved end to end on my own.

04 / Drivetrain

Electric Bike Conversion

Independent Build
● Ongoing
The Constraint

Standard hub-motor e-bike kits are simple to bolt on, but they don't teach you much about drivetrain integration. I wanted a mid-drive conversion instead — the motor driving through the bike's existing gearing rather than the wheel directly, which meant solving belt tensioning, motor mounting, and controller placement instead of just wiring a kit together.

The Approach

I'm modeling the mounting bracket and belt drive layout in Onshape, borrowing parts of the drivetrain thinking from e-skate builds (belt pitch, pulley sizing, tensioner geometry) and adapting them to a bicycle frame's mounting points and clearances.

The Result (so far)

Bracket and belt layout design is in progress, targeting a ### W motor, ##:## belt reduction ratio, and roughly ## mile range per charge. This project is more about the drivetrain fundamentals than a finished product — it's the one where I'm deliberately building slower to understand mid-drive integration properly before I need it on something bigger.

05 / Structural Analysis & Testing

Shockwave Motors

Mechanical Engineering Intern
● Complete
The Assignment

Shockwave needed structural confidence on motorcycle swingarm designs before committing to a build, plus real-world validation of motor controller behavior on a prototype trike — two different problems, one shared need for evidence before manufacturing.

The Approach

I ran FEA on swingarm geometry to check load paths and safety factors under expected riding loads, flagging where geometry changes would meaningfully cut weight without giving up strength. Separately, I reverse-engineered chassis geometry from an existing frame to build an accurate baseline model, and ran motor controller testing on a prototype trike to validate real-world performance against the design intent.

The Result

Structural analysis gave the team a documented basis for swingarm design decisions instead of relying on prior-generation geometry by default — identifying a safety factor of X.X under worst-case loading and a path to cut swingarm weight by roughly X% — and the controller testing surfaced real behavior differences from the spec sheet (response time off by X ms, current draw within X% of rated) — the kind of gap you only find by actually running the hardware.

06 / Industrial Controls

Endeavor Composites

Mechanical Engineering Intern
● Complete
The Assignment

A composites production line needed a PLC control system where none existed — no prior program to modify, no documented control cabinet wiring to inherit. Everything from logic to physical wiring had to be built from a blank slate.

The Approach

I wrote the ladder logic program from scratch on an AutomationDirect Click PLUS PLC, mapping out the sequence of operations the line actually needed rather than porting over a generic template. That meant rewiring the control cabinet to match the new program — running conductors, terminating connections, and validating each input/output against the ladder logic before commissioning.

The Result

A working, documented control system for a production line that previously had none — covering ## I/O points across the cabinet rewire — plus hands-on cabinet wiring experience that translates directly to any manufacturing floor with PLC-driven equipment. Commissioning cut manual process steps by roughly X% and line downtime for changeovers by about ## minutes per shift.
